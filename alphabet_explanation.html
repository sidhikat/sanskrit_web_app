General Introduction

Much time can be devoted to studying the Sanskrit alphabet and all its features. Here, we will discuss the most relevant features for the study of Sanskrit grammar, including its native script, Devanāgarī. The Sanskrit alphabet is a set of sounds (technically known as phonemes) that can be put together to form spoken words. Its writing system is a collection of visual representations of these sounds, arranged into syllables. A “letter” and its sound are not two independent entities in Sanskrit. In English, for example, the letter “a” is an independent entity that contains within it the potential for multiple sounds, such as the “a” in “apple”, “car”, “ace”, or “human”. In Sanskrit, one letter contains only one possible phoneme, so there is a one-to-one mapping of sounds and letters in the alphabet.

The script for writing Sanskrit can be any set of squiggly lines on a page, as long as it accurately represents all the sounds of the alphabet. For visually representing the Sanskrit alphabet, the most common script today is Devanāgarī. Note that different languages employ different types of writing systems. The Sanskrit writing system is known as a syllabic alphabet, alphasyllabary, or abugida. We have generically called it the Sanskrit “alphabet” because that is common parlance.

Although the script can be anything, it is still recommended that you learn Devanāgarī because most of what is written in Sanskrit today can be found in that script. Devanāgarī is not terribly important for representing Sanskrit, considering there is no provision in Sanskrit literature for the codification of its script. Learning it here is more to give you practice so that when you go out into the world of Sanskrit, you can read most of what is written in the language.

At the same time, there are several other scripts used to represent Sanskrit sounds. An increasingly common script being used to represent the Sanskrit alphabet is called IAST, the International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration. This is the script we have been using so far to write Sanskrit words. IAST is an especially important script to learn for Sanskrit, especially if you are learning in the medium of English. Therefore, we should also learn that script along with Devanāgarī.

Now on to the alphabet. A single letter in the Sanskrit alphabet is known as a varṇa or akṣara. These can be of two types: vowel (svara) or consonant (vyañjana).

Vowels - Introduction

A svara is defined in Sanskrit as svayaṁ rājate iti svarāḥ (Literally, “that which rules itself or shines forth by itself is called a svara.”). Those sounds that can exist on their own without the help of any other sound are known as svara. Try saying “a” (pronounced “uh”) and hold the sound in your mouth. Do you feel how it does not need the help of any other sound to exist? On the other hand, try saying the sound “ga” and notice that without the help of the “a”, you would not be able to pronounce the sound “g”. This is how you can recognize something to be a svara. There are nine svara-s in Sanskrit. These are अ (a), इ (i), उ (u), ऋ (ṛ), ऌ (lṛ), ए (e), ओ (o), ऐ (ai), and औ (au). If you have studied Sanskrit before and feel that some vowels (such as आ (ā), ई (ī), etc.) are missing here, don’t worry: we are getting to that now.

In Sanskrit, each vowel has multiple variations depending on how long it is spoken for, which pitch it is spoken at, and whether it is nasalized. These variations allow for there to be a huge number of vowel pronunciations in Sanskrit. Let us take a look at the classifications below.

Vowels - Pitch

Sanskrit vowels have three pitches. They can be spoken at a high (udātta), low (anudātta), or medium (svarita) pitch. The exact frequencies of these pitches are defined only relative to each other, and in Vedic chanting they are heard quite distinctly, because they are preserved there. Classical Sanskrit in its written and spoken form does not preserve pitch differentiation. The high pitch is written with an apostrophe-like mark above the vowel, the low pitch is written with an underscore-like mark underneath the vowel, and the medium pitch is written without any marker.

Vowels - Nasalization

Sanskrit vowels can be either nasalized (anunāsika) or non-nasalized (ananunāsika). Think of this as saying the vowel through your nose, or not, respectively. We might write a nasalized अ in very loose transliteration as something like “unh”. The nasalized vowel is written with a crescent-like marker (known as a candrabindu) on top, like this: अँ.

Vowels - Conclusion

When we put these together, we see that the first four vowels have 18 forms each (3 durations, 3 pitches, and 2 nasalizations), and the rest have 12 forms each (2 durations, 3 pitches, and 2 nasalizations). This is a lot of vowels! But keep in mind, as you go forward, that the 18 forms of अ are not considered separate from अ. They are considered variations of the same vowel. Thus we may refer to all 18 of them with a single अ. All forms of अ are known as the savarṇa letters of अ. Savarṇa literally means “similar letter”, or “letter with similar properties”. Hence all 18 forms of अ are savarṇa with each other, all 18 forms of इ are savarṇa with each other, and so on. This idea also applies to consonants, but in a slightly different way.
